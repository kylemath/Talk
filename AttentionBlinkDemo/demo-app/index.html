The Attentional Blink

Exploring the Temporal Limits of Human Attention

What is the Attentional Blink?

The Phenomenon

The Attentional Blink (AB) is a remarkable limitation in human attention: when trying to detect two targets in rapid succession, people often miss the second target if it appears within 200-500 milliseconds of the first.

Key Finding: "Two episodes of attentional selection cannot occur very close in time"

— Goodbourn et al., 2016

RSVP Paradigm

Rapid Serial Visual Presentation (RSVP) is the experimental technique used to study the attentional blink:

  • Visual stimuli presented sequentially at the same location
  • Rapid presentation rate: 8-12 Hz (83-125 ms per item)
  • Participants identify target items among distractors
  • T1 = First target, T2 = Second target

Temporal Selection & Individual Differences

Not a Universal Limitation

Contrary to early assumptions, the attentional blink is not the same for everyone. Willems & Martens (2016) showed that AB performance varies dramatically across individuals.

Key Factors:

  • Executive Working Memory: Higher capacity → Better AB performance
  • Attentional Focus: Broader focus → Less severe blink
  • Metaphor: "Seeing the bigger picture"

Temporal Precision

Goodbourn et al. (2016) examined not just accuracy, but also the characteristics of errors during the blink.

$$\text{Temporal Selection Quality} = f(\text{Latency}, \text{Precision})$$

Analysis of serial position errors revealed that the AB affects both:

  • Latency: Timing delay in selection
  • Precision: Variability in temporal selection

Spatial Attention vs Conscious Perception

Alef et al. (2020) disentangled the roles of different cognitive processes:

1
Spatial Attention: Neither necessary nor sufficient for the AB
2
Conscious Perception: Necessary but not sufficient
3
Working Memory Encoding: Consciously perceiving a task-relevant event triggers the blink by initiating WM encoding

Neural Mechanisms of the Attentional Blink

Ventral Striatum & Subcortical Gating

Slagter et al. (2017) used rare intracranial EEG recordings to reveal the ventral striatum's crucial role:

80 ms
Early Prediction
Increased alpha (8-12 Hz) and low beta (12-20 Hz) activity
→ Foreshadows T2 miss
200-400 ms
Conscious Access
Increased beta activity (12-30 Hz)
→ Only for consciously perceived targets

Mechanism: The ventral striatum gates cortical information flow, contributing to conscious perception

ERP Components

Zivony & Lamy (2022) synthesized decades of ERP research to identify disrupted processes:

P1 (Intact)

Early sensory processing remains unaffected

N2pc (Disrupted)

Smaller & delayed → Attentional engagement failure

N400 (Intact)

Semantic processing can remain intact

P3b (Absent)

Missing → Working memory encoding failure

Feature-Selective Neural Dynamics

Tang et al. (2020) used multivariate encoding analyses with oriented gratings to track feature-selective information:

$$\text{Neural Selectivity}(t) = \begin{cases} \text{Enhanced} & \text{if target detected} \\ \text{Suppressed} & \text{if target missed} \\ \text{Baseline} & \text{for distractors} \end{cases}$$

Key Insight: The AB involves both short- and long-range neural interactions between competing visual representations vying for conscious access.

Discrete and Continuous Mechanisms

Marti & Dehaene (2017): Dual-Process Model

Using MEG, this groundbreaking study revealed that temporal selection involves two sequential mechanisms:

Stage 1: Continuous Processing

~150 ms

Location: Ventral visual areas

Mechanism: Parallel processing of all images

Effect: Responses to multiple images continuously amplified

$$A(t) = A_0 \cdot e^{\lambda t} \quad \text{where } \lambda = \text{amplification rate}$$
→

Stage 2: Discrete Selection

~350 ms

Location: Visual and parietal areas

Mechanism: All-or-none threshold activation

Effect: Single image isolated for conscious report

$$S(A) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } A > \theta \\ 0 & \text{if } A \leq \theta \end{cases}$$

Continuous to Discrete Transition

Key Implications

Multiple Items Coexist

The brain can process several images in parallel during the continuous phase

Competition for Access

Only one item crosses the threshold for conscious report

Not Just Bottleneck

The AB reflects both gradual amplification and discrete selection

Temporal Integration

Explains why some items can "catch the wave" of an existing attentional episode

Theoretical Models of the Attentional Blink

eSTST Model

episodic Simultaneous Type/Serial Token

The eSTST model proposes that visual attention samples information from discrete temporal episodes, with breaks between episodes characterized by suppressed attention (the attentional blink).

Key Components:

1. The "Blaster" (Transient Attentional Enhancement)

A spatially specific attention boost triggered when task-relevant items are detected.

$$B(t) = B_{\text{max}} \cdot e^{-\frac{(t-t_0)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \quad \text{(Gaussian pulse)}$$

Neural Correlate: N2pc component (posterior-contralateral negativity)

2. Episodic Selection

Attention operates in temporal "chunks" rather than continuously.

$$P(\text{detection}) = \begin{cases} P_{\text{high}} & \text{if } t \in \text{Episode} \\ P_{\text{low}} & \text{if } t \in \text{Blink} \end{cases}$$
3. Type vs Token Processing

Type: Category identification (parallel)
Token: Individual exemplar binding (serial bottleneck)

Signal Detection Model (2024)

Decoupling Sensitivity and Bias

Recent work reveals that the AB specifically impairs perceptual sensitivity while leaving choice bias unaffected.

$$d' = Z(\text{Hit Rate}) - Z(\text{False Alarm Rate})$$ $$c = -\frac{1}{2}[Z(\text{Hit Rate}) + Z(\text{False Alarm Rate})]$$
Sensitivity (d') - IMPAIRED during AB

Measures the ability to discriminate target from non-target

Neural markers: N2p (parieto-occipital), P3

Bias (c) - UNAFFECTED during AB

Criterion for decision-making remains constant

Neural markers: Frontoparietal beta coherence (20-30 Hz)

What Just Happened?

If you missed the second target (T2) when it appeared shortly after the first (T1), you just experienced the attentional blink!

Lag 1 Sparing

When T2 appears immediately after T1 (lag 1), it's often detected because it "catches the wave" of the same attentional episode.

Lag 2-4 Deficit

T2 detection is worst at lags 2-4 (~200-400 ms) when the attentional system is still processing T1.

Recovery

By lag 7-8 (~700-800 ms), the attentional system has recovered and T2 detection returns to baseline.
